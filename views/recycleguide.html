E-Waste Recycling Guide

Learn how to properly dispose of your electronic devices and find recycling centers near you.

How to Recycle Your E-Waste

1

Identify Your E-Waste

E-waste includes computers, monitors, phones, tablets, TVs, printers, keyboards, batteries, and other electronic devices. Identifying what you need to recycle is the first step.

2

Back Up Your Data

Before recycling devices that store data, ensure you've backed up any important information and properly wiped the device to protect your personal information.

3

Find a Recycling Center

Use our recycling center finder below to locate the nearest drop-off points for your electronic waste. Many retailers and manufacturers also offer take-back programs.

4

Properly Package Items

Some items like batteries or screens may need special handling. Check with your recycling center for specific packaging requirements for different types of e-waste.

5

Drop Off Your E-Waste

Take your e-waste to the recycling center during their operating hours. Some centers may offer pickup services for larger items or bulk collection.

What Can Be Recycled?

Almost all electronic devices can and should be recycled. Below are the common categories of e-waste and how to properly recycle them.

Computers & Laptops

  • Desktop computers
  • Laptops and notebooks
  • Tablets and e-readers
  • Computer peripherals

These items contain valuable metals like gold, silver, and copper that can be recovered. Many manufacturers offer take-back programs.

Mobile Devices

  • Smartphones
  • Cell phones
  • Smartwatches
  • Portable music players

Many carriers and retailers offer trade-in or recycling programs. These devices contain rare earth elements that are resource-intensive to mine.

TVs & Monitors

  • LCD/LED televisions
  • Plasma TVs
  • CRT monitors and TVs
  • Computer monitors

Older CRT models contain lead and other hazardous materials. Special handling is required to safely recycle these items.

Printers & Peripherals

  • Printers and scanners
  • Fax machines
  • Keyboards and mice
  • External drives

Printer cartridges can often be refilled or recycled separately. Many office supply stores offer recycling programs for these items.

Home Electronics

  • Audio equipment
  • Video game consoles
  • DVD/Blu-ray players
  • Digital cameras

Consumer electronics contain various metals and plastics that can be recovered and reused in new products.

Batteries & Light Bulbs

  • Rechargeable batteries
  • Single-use batteries
  • CFL light bulbs
  • LED light bulbs

Batteries and certain light bulbs contain hazardous materials that require special handling. Many hardware stores offer recycling for these items.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is it important to recycle e-waste?

E-waste contains toxic materials like lead, mercury, and cadmium that can harm the environment if disposed of improperly. Additionally, valuable resources like gold, silver, and rare earth metals can be recovered from e-waste, reducing the need for environmentally destructive mining.

Is it safe to throw electronics in the trash?

No, it's not safe and in many places, it's illegal. Electronic waste contains hazardous materials that can leach into soil and water when disposed of in landfills. This can lead to environmental contamination and health risks for nearby communities.

How do I protect my personal data when recycling devices?

Before recycling any device that stores data, you should perform a factory reset or use secure data wiping software. For computers, it's recommended to use specialized software that overwrites data multiple times. For smartphones and tablets, perform a factory reset and remove any SIM or memory cards. Some recycling centers also offer certified data destruction services.

Are there any incentives for recycling electronics?

Yes, some manufacturers and retailers offer trade-in programs that provide store credit or discounts on new purchases when you recycle old devices. Additionally, some recyclers may pay for certain high-value items like smartphones or computers in good condition. Check with local programs or use our recycling center finder to identify centers that offer compensation.

What about donating working electronics?

Donating working electronics is an excellent way to extend their useful life before recycling. Many schools, community organizations, and charities accept electronics in good working condition. Be sure to wipe any personal data before donating. If the device is too old or damaged to donate, recycling is the next best option.
